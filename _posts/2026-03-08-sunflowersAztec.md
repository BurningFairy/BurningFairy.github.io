In Aztec culture, the sunflower was a potent symbol of solar energy, war, and royalty. \
Unlike a common garden plant, it was viewed as a physical manifestation of the sun’s power on earth. Its name directly linked its flat, golden disc to the shields of warriors, embodying the bravery required in battle. \
Spiritually, sunflowers were central to the worship of solar deities. Archaeological evidence from the Templo Mayor reveals that the flowers were offered to gods to ensure the sun's continued journey across the sky. Priests and nobles used them in sacred ceremonies, often wearing them as crowns or carrying them as scepters to signal divine authority.
Beyond ritual, they were a practical staple, providing oil and flour for the empire. After the Spanish conquest, the sunflower was suppressed due to its "pagan" associations. Today, it remains a resilient icon of indigenous identity and ancient Mexican heritage
