Sunflowers entered European culture in the early 16th century when Spanish explorers carried their seeds from the Americas to Europe after encountering the plant in 1510. Originally domesticated in North America thousands of years earlier, sunflowers gradually spread across Europe after their introduction. Over time, they gained particular importance in the Russian Empire, where oilseed cultivators developed varieties suited for large-scale production.

During the 18th century, sunflower oil became especially popular in Russia. Its use was supported by religious practices of the Russian Orthodox Church, which allowed plant-based fats during Lent when animal fats were prohibited. This increased demand encouraged the cultivation and improvement of sunflower varieties. In the early 19th century, large-scale extraction of sunflower oil was commercialized in the village of Alexeyevka in Voronezh Governorate by the merchant Daniil Bokaryov, who developed a practical method for industrial production. The crop became symbolically associated with the town, whose coat of arms includes a sunflower. Through these developments, sunflowers became an economically significant plant in European agriculture and culture.
